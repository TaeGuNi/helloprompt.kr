## 🚀 How Real Professionals Write

This is a practical prompt for using LLMs to derive specific scene instructions to be entered into video generation AI (Sora, Runway, etc.) during video planning or storyboarding. Copy the prompt below and fill in the `[Variables]` inside the brackets to suit your situation.

### 🥉 Basic Version

Use this lightly when you need a quick visual description draft for a specific scene.

> **Role:** You are a Hollywood `[Film Director]`.
> 
> **Task:** Create 3 video generation prompts in English that describe the `[Situation]`.

### 🥇 Pro Version

Use this when you need to control everything from physical camera movements and lighting temperature to lens texture with eerie precision.

> **Role:** You are a Hollywood `[Cinematographer and VFX Supervisor]` with 20 years of experience.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Background: `[Highlight chase scene of a Sci-fi thriller movie]`
> - Goal: `[Derive the perfect video generation prompt for Sora or Runway]`
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. Based on the provided scenario, write a video prompt in English containing perfect visual descriptions.
> 2. You MUST include `[Camera Movement (Pan, Tilt, Tracking, etc.)]`, `[Lighting (Cinematic, Neon, etc.)]`, and `[Lens Information (35mm, 85mm, etc.)]`.
> 3. Keep the `[Prompt Length]` concise, around 50 words.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Never use tables; organize in a highly readable bulleted list for mobile accessibility.
> - Actively use professional cinematography terms and optical descriptions.
> - **Bold** important keywords.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - If information is uncertain, do not make it up; answer "I don't know." (Hallucination prevention)
> - Focus on physical and intuitive visual descriptions rather than abstract emotional descriptions that are hard for video AI to understand. (e.g., instead of "sad atmosphere," use "dark back alley with rain, blue neon signs")

## 💡 Writer's Comment (Insight & How to use)

The advancement of this technology is not just an evolution of tools or a temporary trend. We are currently passing through the technical Singularity of visual arts, which heralds disruptive innovation across the entire entertainment industry. The most poignant change is the fact that the **'creator's weapon' has completely changed.**

In the past, great directors or video producers had to physically see and control the optical characteristics of lenses, lighting temperatures, and subject textures on-site. However, the core competency required of us in 2026 is not setting up heavy gimbals or boom mics. It is the **'Prompt Engineering' ability—the skill to use 'Language' as a tool to precisely instruct and manipulate** how AI models render and interpret those complex optical laws and visual elements. This has become the director's vision itself.

The most important "cheat key" I've discovered while refining and testing this prompt hundreds of times in the field is the **maximization of 'Physical & Intuitive Visual Description.'** A fatal mistake many planners make when using video AI is inputting literary and abstract emotions like "a grand scene with a sad atmosphere."

AI does not feel human emotions. To make an AI render 'sadness' perfectly, you must translate it into visual data centered on mise-en-scène: **"<span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">A dark back alley with pouring rain, wet asphalt reflecting blue neon signs, an 85mm close-up shot of a character with a bowed head, cold-toned cinematic lighting.</span>"** The 'Pro Version' prompt provided above is the perfect blueprint that makes the AI perform this tricky 'translation process' itself.

Leading production companies and ad agencies are already adopting this workflow, ruthlessly cutting down dozens of days and hundreds of thousands of dollars in initial setup budgets during the pre-viz stage alone. I guarantee that if you perfectly control just three physical variables—`[Camera Movement]`, `[Lighting]`, and `[Lens Information]`—the quality of your videos will skyrocket from amateur meme-level to the quality of a Netflix Original Series trailer.

Specifically, don't forget to specify the focal length of the lens in your prompt. If you instruct a `[14mm wide-angle lens]`, the AI will show a distorted, grand scale. If you instruct a `[200mm telephoto lens]`, it will create a dramatic out-focusing (bokeh) effect with shallow depth of field. For lighting, instead of just "bright," use specific settings like `[Rembrandt lighting]` or `[Volumetric fog with neon backlighting]` to make the AI start functioning as a Hollywood-grade rendering engine.

Ultimately, whether you are a professional or a solo creator, if you do not internalize this technology and these prompts into your pipeline as a priority, you will eventually lose your place to someone else who can command AI perfectly. This is a cold but unavoidable reality. Don't waste time being vaguely afraid; refine your prompts. That will be the only survival strategy and the most overwhelming weapon in the coming era of video revolution.

## 🧬 Prompt Anatomy (Why it works?)

1. **Assigning a Strong Persona:** Instead of just saying "describe the video," it forces an ultra-expert persona of a **'Cinematographer and VFX Supervisor with 20 years of experience'** onto the LLM, making it spit out optical and cinematic details beyond a generic story.
2. **Control of Specific Physical Variables:** By mandating the inclusion of camera movement, lighting style, and lens specs (mm), it ensures the LLM generates the **'core trigger keywords'** that allow generative video AI models to perform the most dramatic and consistent rendering.
3. **Blocking Fatal Errors (Using Warnings):** Video models understand 'streaming tears and dark lighting with high contrast' much more accurately than 'sad emotions.' By banning abstract emotional expressions and focusing strictly on visual descriptions via constraints, the failure rate for useless outputs is lowered to nearly 0%.
