> _"Is Amazon an e-commerce company or a cloud company? What criteria should I even use for analysis?"_

Amazon is one of the most complex companies to analyze. **AWS** leads the cloud market, **E-commerce** accounts for 40% of U.S. online shopping, and the **Advertising business** is growing rapidly as the third largest behind Google and Meta. When you add **Prime Video**, the **Alexa ecosystem**, and its **own delivery network (DSP)**, evaluating it with a single P/E ratio becomes impossible.

The retail investor's dilemma is this: looking only at AWS, it seems "incredibly cheap," but when combined with the thin margins of e-commerce, it feels like "fair value." Furthermore, because its logistics investment (CAPEX) is so massive, the FCF (Free Cash Flow) is often lower than expected. **Depending on which business you view as the core**, the valuation changes completely. If you value AWS like a cloud stock, it's a buy signal; if you value it like an e-commerce stock, it's a hold... It's a strange situation where the <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">conclusion flips depending on the analysis criteria</span> for the same company.

This prompt uses the SOTP (Sum of the Parts) method to value AWS, e-commerce, and advertising independently and then sums them up to determine whether the "current market cap is at a premium or a discount relative to the sum of its parts."

## ⚡️ 3-Line Summary (TL;DR)

1. **Deconstruct with SOTP:** By evaluating AWS, E-commerce, and Advertising with different multiples, you escape the illusion of the consolidated P/E ratio.
2. **Hidden Value of Advertising:** Valuing the growth as the #3 digital advertising platform after Google and Meta separately reveals significant hidden value.
3. **Beware the CAPEX Trap:** Because logistics and infrastructure investments are massive, you must look at FCF rather than just operating profit to understand the true earning power.

## 💡 Author's Commentary (Insight & How to Use)

The most noteworthy part of this prompt is the **separate valuation of the advertising business**. Many investors see Amazon only as "E-commerce + AWS," but the advertising business has already grown to an annual revenue of $65B. This is larger than YouTube's ad revenue and significant even compared to Meta's total revenue. When you evaluate this business separately using **digital advertising company multiples**, it becomes clear how much of an illusion the "consolidated 60x P/E" can be.

The reason for including the `[FCF vs. Operating Profit]` distinction is that Amazon's biggest investment trap lies here. While operating profit is high, the FCF is lower than expected due to <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">massive capital expenditure (CAPEX)</span> on fulfillment centers and data centers. This prompt is designed so the AI accurately reflects Amazon's characteristic of "earning a lot but investing a lot."

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: How would an AWS spinoff affect Amazon's stock price?**
  - A: If the SOTP analysis shows AWS's standalone value at $880B, that alone is 43% of the current market cap. A spinoff could lead to a **valuation unlock**, potentially raising the stock prices of both entities, though a spinoff is realistically very unlikely.

- **Q: Are ultra-low-cost competitors like Temu and SHEIN a threat to Amazon?**
  - A: I have included e-commerce competition in the prompt's risk analysis. While ultra-low-cost platforms from China are a threat to the price-sensitive consumer segment, it is difficult for them to replace Amazon's core competitiveness: **Prime delivery speed and logistics infrastructure.**
