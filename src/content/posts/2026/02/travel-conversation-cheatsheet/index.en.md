## 🚀 How Real Experts Write This

If you want to quickly extract a list of essential phrases without complex constraints, use the 🥉 Basic Version. If you want a perfectly controlled, customized cheat sheet with fine-tuned nuances and accents, use the 🥇 Pro Version. Just modify the variables in `[ ]` to fit your destination.

### 🥉 Basic Version

> **Role:** You are a local guide and professional interpreter perfectly fluent in the local language and culture.
>
> **Task:** I am traveling to `[Paris, France]` in a few days and don't speak the local language at all. Please organize 10 essential survival phrases used most frequently in restaurants, shopping, transport, and hotels, along with easy-to-read pronunciation guides.

### 🥇 Pro Version

> **Role:** You are a veteran local guide who has lived in `[Fukuoka, Japan]` for over 10 years and perfectly understands the local language, culture, and subtle nuances.
>
> **Context:**
> - Target: I am a Korean traveler going on a 4-day trip to `[Fukuoka, Japan]`.
> - Level: I am a complete beginner who only knows basic greetings like "Hello" and "Thank you."
> - Goal: I want to build a 'Situational Survival Conversation Cheat Sheet' that I can use instantly without opening a translator app.
>
> **Task:**
> 1. Write the essential survival sentences (2-3 per situation) for the following 5 core situations you will definitely encounter during the trip:
>    - Airport/Transport (Asking for directions, ticketing)
>    - Restaurant (Ordering, asking for recommendations, paying)
>    - Convenience Store/Shopping (Stating payment method, refusing plastic bags)
>    - Hotel (Requesting luggage storage before/after check-in, asking for Wi-Fi password)
>    - Emergency/Misc (Asking for restroom location, explaining symptoms when sick)
> 2. The output format must strictly follow the markdown structure below for a clean visual summary:
>
>    **[Situation Name]**
>    - 🇰🇷 Meaning: `[I'll have the same as this]`
>    - 🗣️ **Local Pronunciation:** `[Kore-to onaji mono onegaishimasu]` (Bold for readability)
>    - 📝 Local Script: `[これと同じものお願いします]`
>    - 💡 Pro Tip: `[It's much more natural to say this while pointing at the menu.]`
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Do not use stiff, formal textbook language. Use only the **shortest and most natural colloquialisms actually used by locals in daily life**.
> - Strictly exclude long sentences that are hard to memorize. If a situation can be handled with single words, keep it concise at the word level.
> - Provide the pronunciation in a way that is most similar to how a local would hear it when a non-native speaker reads it aloud.
>
> **Warning:**
> - Avoid literal translations that might sound rude in the local culture. Provide expressions with the most polite yet soft nuance appropriate for the situation.

## 💡 Author's Comment (Insight & How to Use)

The core power of this prompt lies in the constraint: **"Strictly exclude textbook literary language and force the shortest, most natural colloquialisms used by locals."** If you tell a typical translator or AI to translate without context, nine times out of ten, it will output sentences like, "I am terribly sorry, but could you please show me the menu?"—sentences that are too long and stiff to actually say in the field. This is the limitation of AI that learned language from books.

However, in the heat of a busy restaurant, especially with a long line behind you, short and intuitive expressions like "Sumimasen, menu onegaishimasu (Excuse me, menu please)" or "Menu, please" have a much higher success rate. No local expects a traveler to be fluent. If the core words are delivered accurately, they are usually happy to help with a smile.

Don't let these gem-like results from the **Pro Version prompt** rot in your notes app. The system is complete when you fix the results to your smartphone lock screen or widget. You won't panic even if your data SIM doesn't work the moment you step off the plane, or even in a vintage shop in a deep basement. You'll find yourself using the local language at the right moment, just like pulling a card from your wallet.

Furthermore, this prompt allows for perfect **Personalization**. If you have a specific food allergy, try entering in the constraints section: <b>"I have a severe peanut and shellfish allergy, so please add a sentence to strongly emphasize this to the waiter (e.g., I will have to go to the hospital if I eat this)"</b>. Or you could apply it like: <b>"I have difficulty walking, so please add a phrase to ask for the elevator location."</b> Develop it into a one-of-a-kind custom cheat sheet perfectly tailored to your physical characteristics and travel style. Breaking the language barrier and diving deep into local culture takes just 30 seconds.

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: Does it translate smoothly for minority languages (e.g., Mongolian, Icelandic) as well as major languages like English or Japanese?**
  - A: Yes, recently updated high-performance AI models support most languages excellently. However, for rare languages with relatively less training data, a solid tip for getting more natural colloquialisms is to **input the prompt in English first** (`"Act as a Mongolian local guide..."`) to derive the local language, and then ask for the explanation to be translated into your native language.

- **Q: Is the local pronunciation provided by AI similar to actual native speakers?**
  - A: AI provides quite sophisticated pronunciation guides based on vast International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) data. However, due to linguistic characteristics, some sounds are difficult to represent perfectly with just one alphabet (e.g., V, F, Z, etc.). If you add a constraint like <b>"Clearly mark the parts that need stress or higher pitch in bold,"</b> you can get results that are surprisingly similar to actual pronunciation.

This prevents the AI from listing useless formal greetings and forces it to pinpoint only the 'survival sentences' needed to solve immediate problems.
2. **Visual Structuring of Output Format:** We clearly specified the order of output—meaning, pronunciation, local script, and practical tips—and the markdown format. As a result, you get a highly readable cheat sheet UI that fits perfectly on a smartphone screen without additional editing.
3. **Optimization through Colloquialism Constraints:** By setting an explicit constraint for the "shortest and most natural colloquialisms," we blocked the long and awkward sentence structures typical of machine translation. This is the core filter that ensures only practical, easy-to-say sentences survive.
