> _"There are rumors that SpaceX is finally going public... but since it's a private company, there's no financial data, and I have no idea how to estimate its valuation."_

SpaceX is one of the most valuable private companies in the world. Reports suggest its valuation has surpassed **$350B** based on secondary market trading prices. The rapid growth of Starlink (satellite internet), Falcon 9's overwhelming launch market share, and the development of <b>Starship</b>—the largest rocket in human history... it's all incredibly exciting, but the problem is that **publicly available financial data is extremely limited.**

For individual investors, it's crucial to judge <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">when SpaceX will go public</span>, **what it's worth** when it does, whether a Starlink spin-off is likely, and if it's rational to buy immediately after the IPO. However, as an unlisted company, there are no 10-K filings and very few analyst reports.

This prompt is designed to overcome the limitations of a private company by **building an SOTP (Sum-of-the-Parts) valuation based on news and estimated data.** It evaluates Starlink (satellite internet), launch services (Falcon 9/Starship), and Starship-based future businesses (Earth-to-Earth transport, Moon/Mars) separately.

## 💡 Writer's Comments (Insight & How to use)

The core value of this prompt lies in showing that **"even unlisted companies can be analyzed via SOTP."** Many individual investors think of SpaceX as "a great company, so I'll definitely buy when it goes public." However, **for any company, no matter how great, a "fair price" exists.** If it's trading at $350B in the secondary market, you need to know that this price represents a 19% premium over the $294B SOTP base scenario.

The most important variable in unlisted company analysis is **"Selecting Comparable Companies."** The valuation changes significantly depending on who you compare Starlink to. Comparing it to <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">telecom infrastructure (American Tower, T-Mobile)</span> makes an EV/Sales of 8~12x appropriate, but comparing it to high-growth tech (Snowflake, Docker) could justify 20x or more. By asking the AI to "specify the basis for comparables," you ensure transparency in this core assumption.

The most common mistake in IPO investing is **"buying during the first-day excitement."** Looking at past large IPOs (Facebook, Uber, Rivian), a pattern repeats where a 30-50% premium is added immediately after the IPO, followed by a sharp drop around the lock-up expiration (usually 6 months later). This is exactly why I included IPO strategy analysis in the prompt.

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: Is there a way to invest in SpaceX right now?**
  - A: It is currently difficult for individual investors to invest directly. There are three main paths: (a) buying private shares through secondary markets (like Forge Global, often requiring high minimums), (b) indirect investment through funds that hold SpaceX stakes (BAM, Destiny XF, etc.), and (c) subscribing to public shares during the IPO.

- **Q: If only Starlink spins off and lists, what happens to the rest of SpaceX?**
  - A: If Starlink spins off, the core of SpaceX (launch services + Starship) will likely remain private. For investors, this provides an opportunity to invest only in the "stable revenue of Starlink," but you would miss out on the potential value of Starship.
