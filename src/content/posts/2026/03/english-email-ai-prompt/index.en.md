> _"I need to notify an overseas client about a delivery delay. How do I say 'I'm sorry, but...' in English to sound professional? Google Translate sounds so awkward..."_

Business English emails are a source of **chronic stress** for many professionals. What would take 5 minutes in your native language takes over 30 minutes in English. Even with Google Translate, the grammar might be correct, but there's always that nagging feeling that **"a native speaker wouldn't say it this way."** Especially in sensitive situations like handling claims, price negotiations, or rejections, the wrong tone can damage a relationship.

If you instruct AI correctly, you can receive <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">native-level business emails</span> tailored to the situation, relationship, and purpose in just 3 minutes. The key is not simply asking to "translate," but providing context: **"Write an email for this situation, to someone in this relationship, for this purpose."**

```text
Subject: Updated Timeline for Order #2026-0315 — Action Required

Dear Mr. Johnson,

Thank you for your patience regarding Order #2026-0315. I wanted to
provide you with a transparent update on our current timeline.

Due to an unexpected component shortage from our upstream supplier,
we anticipate a revised delivery date of April 1, 2026 — approximately
two weeks beyond our original commitment. Our team is actively working
with alternative suppliers to accelerate this timeline where possible.

To minimize the impact on your operations, we would like to propose:
1. Partial shipment of 60% of the order by March 22
2. Remaining 40% by April 1
3. A 5% discount on the delayed portion as a goodwill gesture

Please let me know if this arrangement works for your team, or if
you'd prefer to discuss alternative options. I'm available for a
call at your convenience.

Best regards,
Jiyoung Kim
Operations Manager, ABC Corp.
```

## ⚡️ 3-Line Summary (TL;DR)

1. **Tone Matching:** AI adjusts subtle business tones like "formal but warm" or "direct but polite" to suit the situation.
2. **Structural Automation:** It automatically follows the standard business email structure: Subject-Opening-Body-Action Items-Closing.
3. **Proposing Alternatives:** It applies professional patterns that offer "solutions and alternatives" rather than just providing a notification.

## 🚀 This is How Real Pros Write

### 🥉 Basic Version

> **Role:** You are a `[Business English Email Expert]`.
>
> **Request:** Write an English email notifying an overseas client of a `[2-week delivery delay]`. Please use a polite and professional tone.

### 🥇 Pro Version

> **Role:** You are a `[Communications Director at a global corporation]`. You have managed email communications with clients in the US, Europe, and Asia for 10 years and are a top expert on cultural nuances and business protocols.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Sender: `[Jiyoung Kim, Operations Manager at a Korean manufacturer]`
> - Recipient: `[Mr. Johnson, US Buyer, 3-year business relationship, annual transaction volume $500K]`
> - Situation: `[Order #2026-0315 delivery delayed by 2 weeks due to component supply issues]`
> - Purpose: `[Notify of delay + Propose alternatives + Maintain relationship]`
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. **Subject Line:** Write a subject line that conveys urgency and professionalism.
> 2. **Email Body:** Include (a) opening thanks, (b) reason for delay (transparently), (c) proposal of alternatives (partial shipment + discount), and (d) next steps (proposal for a call).
> 3. **Tone:** Formal but warm. Apologetic but not subservient; maintain leadership by proposing alternatives.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Keep the email under 200 words (so a busy buyer can read it in 1 minute).
> - Do NOT start with "I'm sorry." Start with a "Thank you" and end with a solution.
> - Present alternatives in a numbered list.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - Avoid expressions that sound like machine translation. Write in a style that a native speaker would find natural.

## 💡 Author's Comments (Insight & How to Use)

The core of this prompt is **"Don't start with Sorry."** While starting with an apology is common in some cultures, it is more professional to start with "Thank you for your patience" in English business emails. Starting with an apology makes you look defensive, whereas starting with gratitude allows you to <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">maintain initiative while expressing goodwill</span>.

Including relationship information in `[Recipient]` is crucial. By specifying "3-year business relationship, annual transaction volume $500K," the AI understands the importance of this relationship and adjusts the tone accordingly. It writes more formally for new clients and with a warmer tone for long-term partners.

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: Can I use AI for sensitive emails like claims or price negotiations?**
  - A: Yes, AI is actually more effective for sensitive emails. It doesn't get emotionally charged and maintains a "firm but fair" tone. However, always have a manager or colleague review it before final transmission.

- **Q: Is it possible to write business emails in Japanese or German?**
  - A: Yes. Just specify the language and culture of the `[Recipient]`. The AI will automatically adjust everything from Japanese honorifics to German Formal/Informal (Du/Sie) distinctions.

## 🧬 Prompt Anatomy (Why it works?)

1. **Cultural Tone Matching:** By specifying the cultures of the sender and recipient, the AI performs constant fine-tuning suitable for that business culture. It automatically chooses the American "Thank you for your patience" over a direct "I am sorry."
2. **The Power of a 200-Word Limit:** Limiting the word count forces the AI to remove unnecessary modifiers and keep only the essentials. In business emails, brevity is professionalism.
