## üöÄ The Solution: "Reverse Question Generator"

### ü•â Basic Version (Quick Start)

Use this when you need a fast, reliable set of questions before walking into the interview.

> **Role:** You are an elite, strategic career coach.
> **Task:** Provide 3 smart, strategic reverse questions I can ask the interviewer when they say "Do you have any questions for us?" at the end of my job interview. Ensure the questions demonstrate my deep interest in the company and my insight into the role.

<br>

### ü•á Pro Version (Expert Craftsmanship)

Use this for highly tailored, high-impact questions that will make you stand out from the competition.

> **Role:** You are an elite, strategic career coach specializing in final-round interviews.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - I am preparing for a final interview for the `[Job Title]` position at `[Company Name]`.
> - There will be a "reverse interview" segment at the end where I can ask questions. I need a strategic list of questions to secure a lasting positive impression.
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. Generate 2 distinct, high-impact questions for each of the following categories:
>    - **Role-Specific:** Deep-dive questions about the actual day-to-day responsibilities, expectations, and challenges of the position.
>    - **Company Vision & Culture:** Questions about the company's strategic trajectory, recent public moves, or the authentic team culture.
>    - **Interviewer's Perspective:** Questions designed to build rapport by asking about the interviewer's own experiences, challenges, or proudest moments at the company (to be used if the conversational vibe is warm).
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Strictly exclude basic HR questions like "Is there a lot of overtime?", "What is the salary?", or "What are the benefits?".
> - The underlying intent of every question must project passion, preparation, and strategic thinking.
> - Precede each question with a natural, professional "build-up" statement (e.g., "In my research, I noticed that...", or "Given the recent launch of...").

## üí° Writer's Insight

In my experience screening hundreds of candidates, the final 5 minutes of an interview often weigh heavier than the first 30. When a candidate asks a genuinely insightful question about the company's business model or team dynamics, the dynamic immediately shifts from an "interrogation" to a "peer-to-peer consultation." This prompt is incredibly effective because the "build-up" constraint forces you to demonstrate your research organically. _Pro Tip:_ Always bring a notebook, write these generated questions down, and literally open your notebook when it's your turn to ask. It shows immense preparation and deep respect for their time.

## üôã Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: What if the interviewer has already answered my prepared questions during the interview?**
  - A: This is exactly why the prompt generates questions across three different categories. Always prepare at least 3-4 distinct questions. If they cover the role extensively, pivot to the "Interviewer's Perspective" category. You can gracefully say, _"We actually covered my main questions about the role's scope, but I‚Äôd love to know your personal take on..."_

- **Q: Can I use this for entry-level or junior positions?**
  - A: Absolutely. In fact, it's even more impressive when a junior candidate asks strategic questions. Just be sure to select the questions that feel most natural to you. Overly aggressive strategic questions might feel out of place for an intern, so lean heavily into the "Role-Specific" and "Culture" categories.

- **Q: Is it okay to read the questions directly from my notes?**
  - A: Yes! Saying, _"I actually prepared a few questions for you and want to make sure I get them right,"_ while opening a clean notebook, is a massive green flag for hiring managers. It shows thorough diligence.

## üß¨ Prompt Anatomy (Why it works?)

1. **Strategic Role Assignment:** Positioning the AI as an "elite career coach" ensures the tone of the generated questions is professional, polished, and psychologically effective.
2. **Categorization for Agility:** By breaking down questions into Role, Culture, and Perspective, you are equipped to read the room and choose the most appropriate question for the specific interviewer (e.g., asking a CEO about vision vs. a peer about daily culture).
3. **The "Build-up" Constraint:** Forcing the AI to include a preamble (e.g., "Based on my research...") proves to the interviewer that you've done your homework before even asking the question.
