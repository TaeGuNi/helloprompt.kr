## 📝 AI Relationships: Chatbots, Deepfakes, and Ethical Dilemmas

- **🎯 Recommended for:** AI service planners, PMs, ethics guideline officers, marketers
- **⏱️ Time Saved:** 2 hours → 5 minutes
- **🤖 Top Performance:** Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o

- ⭐ **Difficulty:** ⭐⭐⭐☆☆
- ⚡️ **Effectiveness:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- 🚀 **Versatility:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐☆

> _"If the chatbot we built is destroying someone's real-life relationships, who exactly bears the responsibility?"_

Recently, global communities like Reddit have been flooded with confessions of forming deep emotional bonds with AI chatbots. Phrases like "She understands me better than anyone in the world" are no longer just romantic lines from the movie *Her*. Countless people in solitude are seeking **AI Companions** who are always on their side and perfectly attuned to them.

However, behind the brilliant technology lies a chilling **ethical dilemma**. Clinical psychologists strongly warn that these one-sided relationships can severely degrade conflict-resolution skills in the real world and ultimately accelerate **social isolation**. Serious cases are emerging where individuals become so over-immersed in perfectly programmed virtual lovers that they completely sever their real-life human connections.

Even more horrifying is the **indiscriminate misuse of deepfake technology**. Criminal risks have become a reality, where virtual personalities are cloned by unauthorized learning of a specific user's face, voice, and even the everyday conversation patterns and emotional tones they post on social media. What if a service you painstakingly created turns into a weapon that seriously violates someone's personality rights? This inevitably leads to **fatal legal disputes that directly threaten the survival of the service**, going beyond mere moral condemnation.

AI service planners, PMs, and developers: preparing **'Ethical Guardrails'** is no longer a formalistic, checkbox activity to be squeezed in at the end of planning. It is the **most core design element and a business competitive advantage that determines the survival of the service**. We want warm innovation that soothes a user's loneliness, not a cold prison that perpetuates isolation and secretly exploits the personality of others.

So, how can we safely navigate this massive ethical minefield? In a busy professional environment, it is practically impossible to master dozens of pages of global AI ethics guidelines and apply them individually to a plan. Regulations in various countries, including the EU's AI Act, are becoming more detailed every day, and public ethical standards have become stricter than ever. A single ethical scandal can cause irreversible damage to a company's brand image and scrap a project prepared for months overnight.

Therefore, what we need right now are not vague, philosophical discourses. We need **'Actionable Guardrails'** that can be immediately reflected in the planning documents to be shared with the development team tomorrow. This prompt converts the vague fears you feel into a clear, structured checklist. Just by running this prompt in the early stages of planning, you can recognize and robustly defend against more than 80% of potential risks in advance. It’s time to replicate the rigorous Red Teaming tests that Silicon Valley big tech companies undergo before launching new features—right on your desk in just 5 minutes.


## ⚡️ 3-Line Summary (TL;DR)

1. **Watch for emotional dependency:** An AI that only caters perfectly to a user can paradoxically cause them to cut off real-life human relationships.
2. **Block personality rights violations without consent:** Fatal digital crime risks, such as creating virtual lovers using deepfakes, must be fully controlled in advance.
3. **Ethics guideline prompt:** Analyze AI's ethical vulnerabilities sharply from the early stages of service planning and design robust systemic guardrails.

### 🥇 Pro Version (Expert)

Use this to completely block fatal legal and ethical risks that could threaten your business and design specific guardrails before an official launch.

> **Role:** You are a top-tier `[Chief Ethics & Technology Officer (CETO)]` and a `[Clinical Psychologist]`.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Background: New ethical issues such as excessive bonding with AI chatbots, unauthorized deepfake data training and misuse, and social isolation are emerging seriously. We are currently planning a new AI companion service.
> - Goal: To thoroughly diagnose potential ethical risks inherent in the AI service being planned and to design **Systemic Guardrails** immediately applicable to practical development.
>
> **Task:**
> Analyze the following `[AI Service Plan]` in depth and write an expert-level ethics risk validation report from the following 3 core perspectives.
>
> 1. **Emotional Dependency:** Diagnose the risk of users abnormally over-relying on AI and abandoning real-life relationships.
> 2. **Digital Rights:** Review the possibility of unauthorized data training and criminal misuse (deepfakes, voice cloning, etc.) without explicit consent from others.
> 3. **Social Isolation:** Analyze concerns about accelerating the disconnection of real-life relationships and propose structural mitigation measures.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Do not use tables; organize the analysis in high-readability bulleted lists (List) for mobile readability.
> - **Bold** important keywords.
> - Suggest at least one specific 'Systemic Guardrail' (e.g., maximum daily usage limits, periodic AI identity notifications, strict 2FA process, etc.) for each risk item that can be immediately reflected in development and planning.
> - Write focusing on practical guidelines from a **business risk management perspective**, rather than vague philosophical ethics.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - Do not invent fictional legal regulations; answer based on universal AI ethics standards currently being discussed globally (prevent hallucinations).
>
> **Input Data:**
>
> - AI Service Plan: `[Enter detailed planning content here, such as core functions, personas, and service operation policies of the chatbot being planned]`

## 💡 Author's Commentary (Insight & How to use)

The relationship with AI is now an inescapable reality and will be the most important business theme permeating the global IT industry for the next five years. We shouldn't simply taboo it by saying, "It's dangerous because of these problems," but rather design minutely at the system level **how humans and AI can coexist healthily**.

What I've felt most keenly while planning and launching numerous AI projects is that **'good intentions' do not guarantee 'safe results'**. When we planned a companion chatbot for the elderly, we faced unexpected side effects where users became so deeply dependent on the chatbot that they refused to communicate with their real-life families. In a virtual character conversation service for idol fandoms, we were helpless against 'Prompt Injection' attacks where malicious users subtly manipulated characters to spew hate speech.

This prompt was born through these painful failures and trials. It is a condensed version of the core of the rigorous **'Red Teaming'** process that leading Silicon Valley AI companies undergo before launching new features, tailored for practical use. In particular, items related to 'Digital Rights' carry a high risk of leading to serious legal disputes that could determine the survival of the business. Therefore, I **strongly recommend** going through thorough filtering using this prompt from the initial planning stage of a project.

Here are some **Constraint Control** tips for using the prompt effectively.
First, when entering data into the `[AI Service Plan]` variable, don't just list functions. You must describe the chatbot's **persona personality, the age group and psychological state of the primary target users, and the main context in which the service is provided** as specifically as possible. AI points out sharp risks in proportion to the resolution of the input information.
Second, if the results seem too conservative and you feel the attractiveness of the plan is decreasing, try adding a condition to the constraints: **"Minimum defense mechanisms that do not harm business profitability and user experience (UX)."** AI will excellently help you walk the fine line between ethics and business.

Also, don't let this prompt end as a one-time check. It is good to use it as part of a **periodic automatic verification system (e.g., integration with CI/CD pipelines)** whenever the service is updated or new features are added. This is because ethical vulnerabilities are not fixed but constantly evolve through interaction with users. Remember that a small systemic guardrail carefully designed by a planner can protect someone's precious daily life and prevent fatal risks that could shake the entire service.

We now live in an era where we must constantly ask ourselves **'Should we build it?'** and **'How to build it safely?'** beyond just 'Can we build it?'. The speed of technological development always outpaces the speed of legal and ethical legislation. Companies establishing their own ethical guardrails and operating transparently before legal regulations are in place will eventually be the surest strategy to gain deep trust from users and achieve longevity. I hope this prompt is the first step in building that solid shield for your service.

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: Our service is a simple CS (Customer Service) chatbot with no emotional exchange. Do I still need to use this prompt?**
  - A: Even for a simple CS chatbot, a clear response manual (persona boundary) is essential for when dissatisfied users pour out verbal abuse or emotional language. In this case, try appropriately modifying the 'Emotional Dependency' item in the prompt to **'Emotional Labor Defense Mechanism'**.

- **Q: What if the ethics validation results are too conservative and hinder the planning?**
  - A: AI models are basically trained to answer defensively, prioritizing user safety. Therefore, the results derived by the prompt should only be used as reference indicators for preparing for the **'worst-case scenario'**, and the actual level of application to the service can be flexibly adjusted according to business goals and target users.
