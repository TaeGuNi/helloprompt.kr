## 🚀 How Real Experts Write Prompts

### 🥉 Basic Version

Use this when you quickly need a basic draft of guidelines.

> **Role:** You are an `[AI Ethics and Crisis Management Expert]`.
>
> **Task:** Write 5 'AI Code of Ethics and Deepfake Prevention Rules' that our company `[Company and Service Name]` must follow.

### 🥇 Pro Version

Use this when you need professional guidelines and crisis response scenarios that reflect the specific context of your service.

> **Role:** You are the Chief AI Ethics Officer and a crisis management expert at a global Big Tech company.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Background: With the recent increase in technology misuse cases like deepfakes and AI crimes, there is significant internal concern about introducing AI features to our service `[Description of Service/Product]`.
> - Goal: Create a draft of strong **AI Ethics Guidelines** and a **Deepfake Misuse Crisis Response Manual** that prioritizes user protection to persuade management and apply to operations immediately.
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. Specifically identify the 3 most fatal AI ethics risks (especially related to non-consensual synthesis and deepfakes) that could occur in `[Description of Service/Product]`.
> 2. Write 5 specific clauses for an 'AI Ethics Guideline' to prevent those risks.
> 3. Organize a 'Step-by-Step Crisis Response Manual (Discovery-Action-Communication-Post-processing)' in a Markdown table for when a user creates or distributes malicious deepfake content using our service.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Write the output in Markdown format. The crisis response manual must maintain a Table format.
> - Focus on specific **Action Items** that practitioners can refer to immediately, rather than abstract moralizing.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - You must include a **Disclaimer** at the end stating that this cannot replace legal advice.

## 💡 Insight & How to Use

Vague terms and conditions that simply say "Do not misuse" provide no legal or practical defense in a real crisis. The part most overlooked by platform companies when launching AI services is the absence of **'specific scenarios and response protocols for potential misuse.'** Even an innovative feature created with good intentions can be exploited by someone to harm others or cause social chaos.

The most powerful weapon of this prompt is that it **forces a simulation of the 'worst-case scenario'**—how you will defend and communicate with the public when your service is exposed to deepfakes or ethical issues. The key here depends on how specifically you describe your business model and target user characteristics in the `[Description of Service/Product]` variable. For example, if it's an anonymity-guaranteed community-based service, the biggest risk should be 'cyberbullying and distribution of deepfake composites targeting specific individuals,' whereas for a service linked with payment features, the context should be controlled to focus on 'AI-based phishing and financial fraud.'

The **Constraints** in the prompt are also placed very strategically. The powerful instruction to "Focus on specific Action Items rather than abstract moralizing" fundamentally prevents the AI from listing obvious and boring ethical codes. Instead, it forces the output of **living instructions** that professionals can immediately refer to and act upon, such as the time it takes for the CS team to blind a post (SLA), the criteria for the policy team to permanently suspend a user account, and the procedures for the PR team to send cooperation documents to investigative agencies.

In particular, the **'Crisis Response Manual'** obtained by running the Pro version should not just be read and forgotten; it must be **documented** in the most visible places like your team's Notion, Slack, or internal Wiki. Large-scale accidents always happen without warning, often on weekends or late at night when the person in charge is away. The speed of the initial response between a team with a clear, specific manual and a team without one directly leads to the critical difference that determines the **survival or demise of the company**, beyond just missing the golden hour.

Furthermore, I strongly recommend conducting a workshop to derive **Edge Cases from various perspectives** by running this prompt together with planning, design, and development department leaders. Each department can perform a Red Team role-play asking, "How can our feature be abused?" and then refine the shield to be thicker and more sophisticated by adding those results back into the prompt's Context and running it repeatedly. Remember that AI ethics is no longer a stale homework assignment for the legal team, but the **earliest stage of planning** that everyone must consider from the moment a product is designed.

## 🙋 FAQ

- **Q: Isn't building deepfake prevention technology the development team's responsibility? Why should planners/marketers use this prompt?**
  - A: Implementing system defense logic is the developer's role. However, the policy criteria for **'what actions to block first'** and **'how to communicate with PR and legal departments when an accident occurs'** must be led by planners and marketers. This prompt builds that solid policy framework.

- **Q: Which AI model is best to use?**
  - A: I strongly recommend **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** or **GPT-4o**, which are overwhelmingly superior in reasoning about complex ethical dilemmas and writing corporate policy documents. They capture actual legal and ethical nuances much more sharply than abstract, obvious words.

- **Q: Can I use the output as-is for external Terms of Service?**
  - A: **Absolutely not.** The draft written by AI is only an excellent 'initial framework' for professional use. Final Terms of Service or externally disclosed codes must undergo thorough review by internal legal teams or professional lawyers to have actual legal effect.

## 🧬 Why it works? (Prompt Anatomy)

1.  **C-Level Persona Assignment:** Instead of a simple AI assistant, it is given the powerful role of **'Chief AI Ethics Officer (CAIO)'**. This induces weighted results from a management and policy perspective rather than obvious moral lecturing.
2.  **Forced Risk Derivation (Red Teaming):** It forces the identification of **'3 fatal risks'** from an attacker's perspective before asking for defense measures. This is designed to let the AI deeply analyze the service's vulnerabilities and establish a defense logic that perfectly corresponds to them.
3.  **Actionable Output:** It strictly excludes vague recommendations like "Be careful" and demands a clear process of **"Discovery-Action-Communication-Post-processing."** This completes a living manual that allows professionals to take immediate action when a crisis occurs.
