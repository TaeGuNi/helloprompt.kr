## 📊 Proven Results: Clear Transformation (Before & After)

### ❌ Before (The Pain We Experienced)

"I look forward to hear from you soon. Please reply me fast."
(A common mistake made in business email closings)

### ✅ After (The Perfect Transformation)

```text
[Revised Version]
"I look forward to hearing from you soon. I would appreciate a prompt reply."

[Correction Notes]
- to hear → to hearing: In the phrase 'look forward to', the 'to' is a preposition indicating direction. Since a preposition must be followed by a noun form (gerund -ing), 'hearing' is the correct grammar.
- reply me fast → appreciate a prompt reply: The verb 'reply' cannot take a direct object (me) without a preposition ('reply to me' is correct). Furthermore, in a formal business context, the word 'fast' can sound rude or overly demanding.

[Native Expression Recommendations]
1. "I would appreciate it if you could get back to me as soon as possible." (Very polite and professional business tone)
2. "Hope to hear from you shortly." (A more casual and friendly everyday business tone)

[Growth Feedback]
- Rating: 6/10
- Key Improvement: Don't forget the absolute rule that a gerund must follow a preposition! In business emails, using 'prompt' or 'as soon as possible' instead of the direct word 'fast' leaves a much more elegant and professional impression.
```

## 🚀 How Real Experts Write

### 🥉 Basic Version

Use this when you are busy and just want to quickly check the correction results and the intuitive reasons behind them.

> **Role:** You are a meticulous and kind `[Native English Tutor]`.
>
> **Request:** Please perfectly correct the grammar of the following sentence and explain 'why' it was wrong in easy-to-understand English. `[Sentence to correct]`

### 🥇 Pro Version (Expert Type)

Use this when you want comprehensive tutoring, including subtle nuance differences, multi-dimensional feedback, and an objective evaluation of your skills beyond simple grammar correction.

> **Role:** You are an expert English writing tutor with over 10 years of experience in professional editing.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - I have written some business and casual English sentences that may contain awkward phrasing or grammatical errors.
> - I don't just want the corrected answer; I want to understand the exact 'why' behind the mistakes so I don't repeat them in the future.
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. **[Revised Version]**: Provide the most natural sentence that perfectly corrects the grammar errors and typos in my text.
> 2. **[Correction Notes]**: Contrast what I wrote with the correction and explain the underlying **grammatical principles** in very simple terms. (e.g., subject-verb agreement, tense, articles, prepositions, etc.)
> 3. **[Native Expression Recommendations]**: Even if my grammar is correct, suggest 2 sophisticated alternatives that native speakers use more frequently in business or daily conversation.
> 4. **[Growth Feedback]**: Evaluate my current writing level on a scale of 1 to 10 and provide a one-line summary of the most important improvement I should focus on.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Avoid using unnecessarily difficult linguistic jargon and explain using intuitive examples.
> - Use bullet points for the [Correction Notes] so the content is easy to scan.
> - Maintain the tone and manner of a kind tutor who encourages the student rather than being cold or overly critical.
>
> **My Sentences:**
> `[Paste the English text you want corrected here]`

Especially when writing to important overseas partners or clients, it's common in practice to produce a sentence that is 100% grammatically flawless but somehow sounds rude or stiff. What we want isn't a thesis-level grammar explanation, but intuitive feedback we can use in an email tomorrow.

Also, forcing the use of **bullet points** in the `[Correction Notes]` module is a key optimization. In a mobile environment or during a busy workday, no one has time to read a "Wall of Text." By forcing the format, I've maximized information readability so you can scan and digest the data instantly.   - A: Yes, it is perfectly compatible. By simply changing the role in the first line to `[Native Japanese Tutor]`, you can receive deep grammatical explanations at the JLPT level and even perfect recommendations for tricky business honorifics (Keigo).

- **Q: Is it okay to put an entire long email body in at once instead of just one or two sentences?**
  - A: Absolutely. However, if the text is long, try adding a specific constraint at the bottom like `(Please maintain the tone and manner of the entire text as a [Polite Business Email])`. You will get much more consistent and smooth editing results.

- **Q: The correction results provided by the AI feel too vast and complex.**
  - A: If advanced items like [Growth Feedback] or [Native Expression Recommendations] feel overwhelming right now, simply delete those specific instruction lines from the Pro version prompt. You'll get a very clean and intuitive 'Core Grammar Note' quickly.
