```text
[Variable Extraction]
- h (Initial height) = 45 m
- v_0 (Initial velocity) = 0 m/s (Derived from the expression 'dropped')
- v (Final velocity) = ?

[Hidden Conditions]
- Air resistance and friction are ignored (Assumed ideal vacuum since not specified).
- a (Acceleration) = g (Gravitational acceleration, ≈ 9.8 m/s^2). Direction set as positive (+) downward.

[Strategy Development]
The information provided in the current problem completely lacks the time (t) variable. Since the question asks for the change in velocity (v) relative to displacement (h), the most rational and fastest approach is to use the 'Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy' or the 'time-independent kinematic equation' (the 3rd equation of motion).

[Equation Modeling]
Applying the kinematic equation for constant acceleration (time-independent):
2as = v^2 - v_0^2

Substituting variables identified for the context (s = h, a = g, v_0 = 0):
2gh = v^2 - 0
∴ v = √(2gh)
```

## 💡 Author's Insight (Strategy & Usage)

The true value of this prompt lies in focusing the AI on **'designing the blueprint'—translating complex text problems into sophisticated mathematical models**—rather than treating it as a simple 'calculator.'

Most LLMs (Large Language Models) boast world-class capabilities in natural language processing and textual reasoning, but they often suffer from fatal hallucinations when solving complex arithmetic or multi-step algebraic equations. They might omit numbers or flip signs—mistakes that seem absurd to humans. However, they show insights far beyond humans in analyzing linguistic clues and building logical frameworks. I have isolated and utilized these strengths by separating them from the AI's weaknesses. Instead of making the unreasonable demand to calculate the final answer, I demand a **'pure mathematical model'** that strictly excludes numbers, thereby cutting off the root of calculation errors. This is the essence of prompt engineering: **Constraint Control**.

As a result, users can internalize the principles 100% by substituting numbers with their own hands onto the solid equation framework provided by the AI. You will experience overwhelming performance and time savings, especially in **mechanics** when you're unsure whether to use momentum conservation or energy conservation, or in **electromagnetism** when you need to set numerous boundary conditions. Beyond mere problem solving, it helps in understanding the fundamental mechanisms of how physical laws apply to real-world situations. A top-down approach that understands the structure of the problem first dramatically reduces errors and increases the accuracy rate.

To use this prompt even more powerfully in practice or study, it's better to include the **parts where you are stuck or points you suspect** in the `[Input]` variable, rather than just throwing in the problem text. For example, ask: "Problem content: [Pasted text] / Note: I'm not sure if I can really ignore air resistance in this problem or if I should consider terminal velocity." ## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: What should I do if the equations look like broken gibberish in the output?**
  - A: This is because the AI used **LaTeX syntax** to render equations according to the prompt constraints. While it looks clean in most major AI service environments (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.), if it appears broken in an environment that doesn't support rendering, simply ask: <b>"Rewrite the formulas in Plain Text format."</b>

- **Q: Can't I just tell it to calculate the final answer at once?**
  - A: Yes, you can. You can remove the sentence "Focus on creating a blueprint" from the constraints and change it to: <b>"Calculate and derive the final answer perfectly."</b> However, as the difficulty of the problem increases, so does the probability of the AI making a calculation error. I strongly recommend verifying whether the logical equation itself provided by the AI is correct first.

- **Q: Does this work for advanced undergraduate physics (Quantum Mechanics, Electrodynamics, etc.)?**
  - A: Yes, it shows excellent performance. However, since symbols and boundary conditions become very complex at the undergraduate level, adding specific academic context to the `[Context]` section, such as <b>"This is a 3rd-year undergraduate Electromagnetics problem,"</b> will help you get much sharper and more sophisticated answers.
