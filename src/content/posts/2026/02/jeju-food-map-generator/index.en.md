## 💡 Author's Commentary (Insight & How to use)

The secret to successfully writing a restaurant search prompt lies in **'Negative Prompting'** and **'Cross-Validation'** across different platforms.

The most common mistake people make when using AI is giving vague, positive instructions like "Recommend some pretty and delicious cafes in western Jeju." In this case, the AI will prioritize scraping the massive volume of viral ads exposed most frequently on the internet—those with large marketing budgets. Consequently, you end up seeing the same 'Billboard List' of ads you'd find on a search engine, just inside a chatbot window.

To prevent this disaster, we must place **strong constraints** on the prompt. The key is to explicitly instruct the AI to **exclude** disclaimer keywords prevalent in the Korean blog ecosystem, such as "small manuscript fee," "provided the product," or "sponsored." This forces the AI to significantly lower the weight of, or entirely ignore, pages containing these words. This cleanly removes <b>noise data</b> at the first stage.

The second secret weapon is **cross-validation between platforms**. The gourmet rating ecosystem in Korea has distinct characteristics for each platform. Naver tends to have overall rating inflation because of active reservation and review events. Conversely, Kakao Map users tend to be very cold and critical in their evaluations, while Google Maps provides a mix of objective indicators from both foreign tourists and locals. Therefore, relying on a score from a single platform is dangerous. As seen in the prompt, setting a specific cross-condition like <b>"Google Maps rating 4.2+ AND Kakao Map rating 4.0+"</b> allows you to perfectly filter out fake hot spots whose scores were manipulated on only one platform through viral marketing.

Furthermore, for practical use, the choice of AI model is crucial. I strongly recommend using **Perplexity AI** or **ChatGPT's Search feature**, which are integrated with real-time web browsing, rather than standard models that only learned from static past data. This is because current closures, recent changes in operating hours, and the latest review trends from last month can only be accurately identified through real-time web search.

One more tip for controlling the `[ ]` variables: When setting the target area, instead of just saying 'Jeju Island,' **set the geographical range as narrowly as possible (micro-targeting)**, such as 'Hanrim-eup Coastal Road, Jeju' or 'The backstreets of Aewol Cafe Street.' As the range widens, the AI tends to revert to its instinct of suggesting famous, cliché tourist-only restaurants. If you master and apply these details of prompt engineering, you will no longer be fooled by 'Instagrammable' spots that are only flashy on the outside, and you'll be able to discover true local gems that residents genuinely cherish.

## 🧬 Prompt Anatomy (Why it works?)

1. **Cross-Validation:** It forces the AI to simultaneously meet strict rating criteria from multiple platforms with different tendencies, such as Google Maps and Kakao Map. This perfectly filters out restaurants that have intentionally manipulated ratings on a specific platform.
2. **Explicit Ad-Filtering:** It instructs the AI to explicitly exclude specific sponsored keywords unique to the Korean blog ecosystem, like 'small manuscript fee' and 'sponsored.' This prevents the AI from misidentifying contaminated review data as positive evaluations.
3. **Practical Metadata Requirements:** It's designed to force the output of core information essential for actual travel planning, such as 'Presence of private parking' and 'No-kids zone status,' rather than just 'taste' evaluations, maximizing on-site utility.
