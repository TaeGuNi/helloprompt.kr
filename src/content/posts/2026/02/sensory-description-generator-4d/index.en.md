> _"He entered the dark forest. He was scared." — This is the fatal reason readers drop your story first._

'Show, Don't Tell.' This is the number one rule that anyone starting to write has seen hundreds of times in writing guides. However, when we actually sit down in front of a white monitor with a blinking cursor, the rich imagination swirling in our heads often vanishes. Instead, we are left with the painful experience of writing **dry, flat explanations** like "The weather was cold," "The atmosphere was scary," or "The heroine was beautiful." No matter how fascinating the world-building or breathtaking the narrative you've spent all night creating, it’s useless if you can't 'show' it to the reader. Readers lose interest and hit the back button or close the book at the sight of a single bland description.

This pain is particularly acute for those in professions that must capture a reader's attention in a short time, such as **web novel writers** or **copywriters**. How many times have you stared at your keyboard because the perfect scene in your head wouldn't translate into the right words, even as the deadline approached? When you start piling on adjectives to convey emotion, the writing quickly descends into a tedious and tacky list of 'explanations.' To pull the reader into the world you've created, you must go beyond mere reporting and stimulate the **'five senses' (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch)** to paint a vivid image in their mind.

Readers feel nothing when faced with familiar expressions they've read thousands of times. They want to stand in the rain with your protagonist, smell the damp earth, and hear the ragged breathing. But extracting all these sensory details from the human brain at once is no easy task, even for highly trained veteran authors. It is even more of a harsh punishment for professionals who must handle massive daily serializations or complete a product landing page copy by tomorrow morning. Time is short, inspiration is dry, and the pressure of the deadline is suffocating. Are you struggling in this vicious cycle, draining your precious talent and passion?

## 🚀 How Real Professionals Write

This prompt was perfected after dozens of trials and errors. Copy the prompt below and fill in the `[Variables]` in brackets to suit your situation and put it to work immediately.

### 🥉 Basic Version

Use this for light sketches of ideas in your head.

> **Role:** You are a pure literature author with extremely rich expressiveness and a master of landscape description.
>
> **Task:** Transform the plain explanatory sentences I provide into multi-dimensional descriptions where the five senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) come vividly to life.
>
> **Input:** `[A tired commute home in the rain]`

### 🥇 Pro Version

Deploy this when you need overwhelming immersion and delicate detail that pulls the reader along.

> **Role:** You are a genius novelist who stimulates the reader's five senses and an excellent stylist who excels at capturing subtle and complex atmospheres in text.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Background: The draft sentences are too dry and 'telling,' seriously hindering the reader's immersion.
> - Goal: Elevate these sentences into the realm of 'showing,' where the reader can vividly experience the scene as if they were standing there themselves.
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. Based on the `[Input Sentence]` below, rewrite it using at least 3 of the **five senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)**.
> 2. Provide 3 different versions of the transformation, perfectly matched to the `[Desired Atmosphere]` options.
> 3. Use brackets for the `[Variable]` sections so the user can fill them in.
>
> **Variable Input:**
>
> - **[Input Sentence]:** Entered an old haunted house. It was dusty and smelled.
> - **[Desired Atmosphere]:**
>   1. Horror/Thriller (Breathtaking tension, sticky discomfort, unknown threat)
>   2. Emotional/Languid (Traces of faded time, profound loneliness, forgotten memories)
>   3. Mystery/Fantasy (Strange dissonance, hidden ancient secrets, magical aura)
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - 🚫 Forbidden Words: Strictly prohibit the use of adjectives that directly name and force emotions or sensations, such as "scary," "sad," "happy," "cold," etc.
> - Do not describe emotions one-dimensionally; instead, lead the reader to infer the emotion through subtle changes in the environment or physical reactions (e.g., hair standing on end, trembling breath, cold sweat).
> - Completely exclude tired clichés (e.g., 'pitch-black darkness', 'heart pounded') and use fresh metaphors and analogies that catch the eye.
> - To ensure mobile readability, never use tables; organize in a high-readability list format.
> - Be sure to **bold** important keywords.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - Do not make up information that is not certain; answer "I don't know." (Hallucination prevention)

## 💡 Creator's Comments (Insight & How to Use)

The true destructive power of this prompt lies not in simply 'lengthening the sentence' or 'decorating it with fancy modifiers.' The core is providing the reader with a **'perfect shift in perspective.'** We often make the mistake of summarizing a situation from an observer's point of view when translating an image into text. However, the moment you apply this prompt, the sentence's camera moves from a hovering omniscient view right into the protagonist's pupils, onto their skin, and close to their ears. I pull this prompt out as my most powerful secret weapon during **climax scenes** where tension explodes in a web novel, or at decisive moments in product landing pages where I need to dramatically and sensually convey the consumer's offline experience.

The most highly recommended strategy for utilizing this prompt to 120% in practice is the **'Cherry-picking'** technique. AI-generated descriptions can sometimes be overly flowery or smell of translationese. At this point, copying and pasting the AI's first result verbatim is what amateurs do. A true master exquisitely extracts only the sharp words, expressions, or fragments of metaphors that strike their heart and refines them into their own style.

The true value of AI is not as a vending machine for finished manuscripts, but as a **warehouse of inspiration for collecting sharp 'sensory vocabulary'** that you hadn't thought of. For example, if the AI transforms a plain "It smelled" into "the metallic scent of rust" or "the stench of damp, sticky mold piercing the lungs," just obtaining one or two of those raw words completely changes the quality and density of your entire sentence. This is because the AI throws out an infinite number of ingenious synesthetic images that are difficult to reach with a human's limited vocabulary.

Furthermore, depending on how you control the **`[Desired Atmosphere]`** variable, this prompt takes on a thousand faces. Even when describing the same space, the adjectives and verbs chosen by the AI will be completely different if you input "profound depression and sense of loss" versus "bizarre and surreal dreaminess." The forbidden word settings in the **Constraints** are also very important. By fundamentally banning cheap words that directly expose emotions like "scary" or "sad," the AI is forced to follow the principle of **'Show, Don't Tell.'** The reader becomes infected with sadness themselves through the description of the protagonist's trembling fingertips and the cooling air, without the word "sad" ever being used. This is the true writing secret of real authors who hack the reader's brain, and it is the overwhelming power this prompt will put in your hands.

## 🙋 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

- **Q: What if the description is too long and wordy, like a translation?**
  - A: Try adding a condition to the prompt constraints like `- Keep the word count under 150 characters and write with tight tension.` Or, you can clean up unnecessary modifiers by specifying a specific author style, such as "Write in a dry, hard-boiled style like Ernest Hemingway."

- **Q: Can this be used for product landing page copywriting (e-commerce) as well as novels?**
  - A: Absolutely! Set the `[Desired Atmosphere]` variable to something like "freshness, coolness, overwhelming trust," and input "Applying this moisture cream keeps skin hydrated all day" as the `[Input Sentence]`. It exerts tremendous power in extracting sensual and fatal selling points that open consumers' wallets.

- **Q: Which AI model is the smartest for generating literary descriptions?**
  - A: For capturing subtle literary nuances and creating metaphors that aren't obvious, **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** currently boasts overwhelming performance that is peerless. On the other hand, ChatGPT (GPT-4o) tends to output somewhat standardized and textbook metaphors, so I strongly recommend Claude for emotional writing.

## 🧬 Prompt Anatomy (Why it works?)

1. **Banning Direct Emotion Words (Negative Prompt):** By strictly prohibiting the use of direct words like "scary," the AI is forced to scrape together visual and tactile replacement words (e.g., 'hair standing on end', 'frozen air piercing the lungs') to continue the sentence. This is the core mechanism for making the AI recognize 'Show, Don't Tell.'
2. **Forced Multiple Options (3 Versions):** It is designed to extract 3 different atmospheric tones with a single prompt execution. Users can cherry-pick only the descriptions that perfectly match their planning intent, extremely shortening work time.
3. **Specific Sensory Targeting (Minimum of 3):** Instead of giving vague instructions like "write well," explicitly pointing out the human senses mechanically yet surely raises the three-dimensionality and resolution of the sentence.
