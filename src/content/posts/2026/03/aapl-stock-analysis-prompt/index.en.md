## 🚀 How the Real Experts Write It

This is a prompt perfected through dozens of trials and errors. Copy the prompt below and fill in the `[Variable]` parts to match your situation for immediate professional use.

### 🥉 Basic Version

> **Role:** You are a `[Stock Analyst]`.
>
> **Request:** Analyze `[AAPL]` stock. Based on recent financial data, explain whether a Buy/Sell/Hold decision is appropriate, along with the supporting evidence.

### 🥇 Pro Version

> **Role:** You are a `[Senior Equity Analyst at a Top-Tier Wall Street Investment Bank]`. You hold a CFA charter and have over 10 years of experience covering the tech sector.
>
> **Context:**
>
> - Target for Analysis: `[AAPL (Apple Inc.)]`
> - Time of Analysis: `[As of March 2026, utilize the latest data]`
> - Investor Profile: `[Retail investor with a long-term investment horizon, 1–3 years of investment experience]`
>
> **Task:**
>
> 1. **Financial Fundamental Analysis:** Based on the results of the last four quarters, summarize revenue, operating margin, FCF, dividends, and share buybacks. Pay particular attention to the trend in the revenue share of the Services segment.
> 2. **Valuation Assessment:** Present the Forward PER, PEG Ratio, and EV/EBITDA, and compare the current levels against the 5-year average. Apply a simple DCF model to calculate a fair value range.
> 3. **Technical Analysis:** Judge the short-term trend based on the 200-day moving average, RSI (14-day), MACD, and Bollinger Bands. Specify key support and resistance levels.
> 4. **Comprehensive Investment Judgment:** Synthesize the above analysis to provide an investment rating (Strong Buy / Buy / Neutral / Sell / Strong Sell) and a 12-month price target. Present three major risk factors and three catalysts each.
>
> **Constraints:**
>
> - Every numerical figure must be accompanied by supporting evidence (source or calculation logic).
> - Do not use tables; organize the information using bullet points (lists).
> - Explicitly state at the end that this is for **educational and analytical purposes** and not an investment recommendation.
>
> **Warning:**
>
> - Do not invent figures or predictions that are not certain; mark them as "Data verification required." (Anti-hallucination)
> - Avoid overly optimistic or pessimistic bias and present both scenarios in a balanced manner.

## 💡 Insight & How to use

The core design principle of this prompt is **"Separation of the 4 Stages of Analysis."** Many people ask AI a single line like "Analyze AAPL" and end up disappointed. Of course they do. That's like telling a doctor, "Give me a check-up." Just as a precise diagnosis requires specific details about where it hurts, what tests you want, and your medical history, stock analysis requires clearly separating the four stages: **Financials → Valuation → Technicals → Comprehensive Judgment**.

The `[Investor Profile]` variable is particularly important. If you specify a "long-term investment retail investor," the AI focuses on <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">long-term growth stories and dividend sustainability</span> rather than short-term trading signals. Conversely, if you change it to "short-term swing trader," it analyzes with a heavier weight on technical analysis and short-term momentum. Even for the same stock, **the analysis should change completely depending on who is asking**.

The condition in `[Constraints]` to "accompany figures with evidence" is the **key to anti-hallucination**. When an AI says, "The PER is 25x," it's meaningless if you can't verify whether that number is based on the latest earnings or data from a year ago. By forcing the inclusion of evidence, the AI will self-flag uncertain figures as "Data verification required." This alone can **drastically reduce investment mistakes** based on incorrect numbers.

One more pro tip: don't just put **AAPL alone into the `[Target for Analysis]`—try adding a competitor comparison**. For example, by expanding it to "Comparative analysis of AAPL vs. MSFT vs. GOOGL," you can grasp **relative investment attractiveness** using the same analysis framework. By extracting relative valuations within a sector, you get much sharper insights—not just whether "Apple is good or bad," but "Which stock is currently the most undervalued among the Big Tech Three." This is how retail investors can use AI to take a step closer to the analytical capabilities of institutional investors.

## 🙋 FAQ

- **Q: Can I trust the price target provided by the AI?**
  - A: You should never trust it blindly. AI analysis is based on **data up to its training cutoff**, so the latest earnings reports or news might not be reflected. Use AI analysis as a **tool to gain an analytical framework** (the perspective from which to view the stock), and always make final Buy/Sell decisions after directly checking the latest data yourself.

- **Q: Can I use the same prompt for Korean stocks (Samsung Electronics, Naver, etc.)?**
  - A: Yes, it works the same way if you just change the `[Target for Analysis]` variable to "Samsung Electronics (005930.KS)." However, you will get a much more sophisticated analysis if you additionally specify Korean market characteristics (high dividend tax, governance discount, KOSPI valuation discount, etc.) in the `[Context]`.

## 🧬 Why it works?

1. **The Power of 4-Stage Analysis Separation:** By separating Financials → Valuation → Technicals → Comprehensive Judgment, the AI performs a deep, independent analysis at each stage. Asking for an "analysis" all at once leads to a superficial overview, but dividing the stages creates **professional-level density** in each area.
2. **The Magic of the Investor Profile Variable:** Telling the AI "who the analysis is for" completely changes the **direction of interpretation**, even for the same data. It emphasizes the long-term growth story for long-term investors and chart signals for short-term traders. This is the core of personalized analysis.
