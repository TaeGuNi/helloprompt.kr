> _"Oil prices rose on news that OPEC is extending production cuts, then fell again on announcements of increased US crude inventories... There are so many variables moving the price that I don't even know where to start my analysis."_

Predicting West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices is **much more difficult** than stock analysis. While stocks often require analyzing a single company's financial statements and industry trends, oil prices are influenced by **entirely different dimensions of variables** acting simultaneously: **geopolitics (Middle East conflicts, Russian sanctions)**, **supply and demand (OPEC+ cuts, US shale production)**, **macroeconomics (dollar strength, Chinese demand)**, and **technology (EV transition, renewable energy)**.

For individual investors, daily news often seems contradictory. One headline says "OPEC cuts → prices rise," followed immediately by "US Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) release → prices fall." "China's economic recovery → demand increase" and "Expansion of EV adoption → long-term demand decrease" appear in headlines on the same day. <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">Weighting and synthesizing all these variables to make a comprehensive judgment</span> is nearly impossible using human intuition alone.

This prompt separates the variables moving oil prices into **"Supply Side" and "Demand Side,"** assigns a direction (upward/downward pressure) and intensity (1–5) to each variable, and creates a **score-based prediction framework**. Instead of predicting by gut feeling, you can structurally determine whether "upward pressure is stronger" or "downward pressure is dominant" by summing the weighted variables.

## ⚡️ TL;DR

1. **Supply-Demand Scoring Framework:** By assigning direction and intensity to each variable, you can quantitatively compare "upward force vs. downward force."
2. **Simultaneous Supply-Demand Analysis:** Looking only at OPEC+ or only at demand leads to bias. You must analyze both sides simultaneously for a balanced outlook.
3. **Scenario Ranges:** Instead of asking "what will the price be," suggest "what range will it be in" to manage risk.

## 💡 Insight & How to Use

The core design of this prompt is the **"Variable-based Scoring System."** The biggest trap in oil price analysis is **getting tunnel vision on a single variable**. If you simplify it to "Oil prices will rise because OPEC is cutting production," you ignore the opposing force of increasing US shale production. By assigning a 1–5 intensity to each variable and summing them up, you can <span style="color:var(--color-cyber-cyan)">quantitatively compare</span> how strong the forces on both sides truly are.

In practice, the most useful way to use this is to **update it weekly**. After an OPEC meeting, you might raise the production cut intensity from 3 to 4; after US inventory data is released, you adjust the shale supply score. By accumulating these updates, you can capture **trend changes**, such as "upward pressure has become stronger/weaker compared to last month." This is the difference between simply reacting to news headlines and conducting structural analysis.

## 🙋 FAQ

- **Q: Can AI accurately predict oil prices?**
  - A: Neither AI nor anyone else can "accurately" predict oil prices. The purpose of this prompt is not prediction, but **setting scenario ranges**. Having a range like "Bearish $65, Base $78, Bullish $92" allows you to prepare investment strategies suited for each scenario in advance.

- **Q: Will oil prices crash if EV adoption accelerates?**
  - A: While it is a structural factor for demand reduction in the long term, its impact is limited in the short term (2026–2030). Most global oil demand comes from trucks, ships, aircraft, and petrochemicals, where decarbonization is still in its early stages.

## 🧬 Anatomy of the Prompt (Why it works?)

1. **Objectivity of Score-based Summation:** By quantifying each variable on the same scale (1–5), you reach an **arithmetic conclusion**, such as "OPEC cuts (4 points) vs. Shale increase (3 points) → Net supply decrease (+1 point)." This leaves less room for emotional bias.
2. **Dual Structure of Supply and Demand:** Looking at only one side leads to bias. By forcing a simultaneous analysis of both sides, you don't miss **important offsetting effects**, such as "supply is decreasing, but demand is also slowing down."
